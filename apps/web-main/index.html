How It Works

What K1 Actually Does

You press play. Within a single audio frame, the bar reacts—not just to loudness, but to the actual frequencies in your track. Bass swells from the center, highs shimmer at the edges, and the light looks like it's floating inside the glass instead of sitting on top.

Under the hood, a dual edge-lit light guide injects light from both sides into optical-grade acrylic while an ESP32-S3 runs 96-band analysis at 125 Hz to drive those internal waves. You never see individual diodes, just a continuous volume of light that makes your room look like your music sounds.

The Difference

Not Another RGB Bar

Most "sound reactive" bars just flip between a handful of presets when they hear volume changes—rainbow chases, fake VU meters, everything slightly behind the beat.

K1 does what they don't:

  • Other bars react to volume. K1 reacts to music.
  • They hear "loud." K1 hears 96 frequencies.
  • Single-digit-millisecond engine tick means light lands with the beat, not after it.
  • They ship 18 color modes. K1 ships a physics-inspired engine.

And because it drives a light guide instead of bare LEDs, you never see "a strip of dots"—you see light suspended inside the panel, the kind that makes people ask, "Where is that even coming from?"

The Hardware

Inside the Bar

  • Dual edge-lit light guide plate with 320 addressable RGB LEDs
  • ESP32-S3 running the audio + render loop
  • 96-band analysis at 125 Hz
  • Single-digit-millisecond internal engine tick for near-instant response
  • I²S MEMS mic capture (line-in planned for future configs)
  • USB-C power for sane desktop and studio setups
  • Pre-loaded physics-inspired modes, plus firmware you can extend if you want to hack
